Solar Car at Virginia Tech - Mechanical Design Engineer

Ergonomical Steering Wheel Development

As part of Virginia Tech's Solar Car team, I contributed to the design and development of Sun Voyager, a fully solar-powered race car built for international endurance competitions. My primary project focused on redesigning the steering wheel through an iterative, driver-centered engineering process involving hand sketches, CAD development, rapid prototyping, and continuous driver feedback.

01 - The Challenge

Driver Experience

The existing Formula 1-inspired wheel kept the cockpit compact, but its rectangular layout reduced steering leverage and became uncomfortable during endurance driving. The redesign needed better ergonomics without disrupting the existing steering interface.

02 - Concept Development

Sketching and CAD Design

Initial concepts explored wheel geometry, display integration, and mounting features before being translated into SolidWorks. These early models established the foundation for physical prototypes and driver feedback.

03 - Driver-Centered Iteration

Prototype, Test, Refine

Driver testing guided the evolution of the design. Prototype diameters of 270 mm and 310 mm bracketed the target before converging on a 285 mm final design. Thumb-reach studies refined control placement across more than 35 CAD iterations.

04 - Final Design

Manufactured and Driver Tested

35+ CAD iterations
285 mm Final diameter
Driver tested Physical validation

The final wheel improved comfort, steering leverage, and control accessibility while integrating with the Sun Voyager cockpit.

SolidWorks Ergonomic Design Rapid Prototyping Driver Validation

Solar Car at Virginia Tech - Structural Validation

Steering Assembly FEA Validation

Validated the Sun Voyager steering assembly before manufacturing by combining SolidWorks CAD, finite element analysis, and engineering calculations to verify the structural integrity of the rack mount assembly and custom weld tabs under conservative loading conditions.

01 - The Challenge

Verify the load path before manufacturing

The steering assembly transfers driver input through the wheel, rods, U-joints, and rack into the front suspension. The rack mount and welded tabs needed to withstand steering loads while maintaining an appropriate safety margin.

02 - Steering Assembly

Model the complete system

The complete steering system was modeled and integrated in SolidWorks before structural validation, with particular focus on the custom rack mount assembly and welded steel tabs.

1Steering wheel 2U-joints 3Steering rod 4Rack mount 5Weld tabs
03 - Finite Element Validation

Test the conservative load case

Finite element analysis was performed on the rack mount weld tabs using ASTM A656 Grade 80 HSLA steel. A conservative 150 N load applied to each side produced stress below the material yield strength.

204.8 MPa Maximum von Mises stress
551.6 MPa Material yield strength
2.69 Factor of safety at 150 N
04 - Engineering Conclusion

Safety margin increased at operational loads

Because the 150 N load represented an exaggerated case, lower operational loads were predicted to produce significantly higher safety margins. The validated design progressed into manufacturing and vehicle integration after the structural assessment.

Aerospike Film-Cooling CFD

01
Why it matters

Unlike a bell nozzle, an aerospike linearizes exhaust expansion for higher thrust, lower losses, and better fuel use.

02
The bottleneck

The same hot exhaust that creates thrust can overheat the nozzle wall.

03
Cooling idea

Inject a thin coolant layer along the wall before heat reaches the structure.

04
Future direction

Find the best flow rate and coolant for performance, weight, and sustainability.

5.5-30 kg/s Coolant mass-flow range studied
540.75 K Average wall temperature at 14.4 kg/s
327.34 K Average wall temperature at 30 kg/s
  • 2D Fluent setup with energy equation and SST k-omega turbulence modeling.
  • 2700 K exhaust flow paired with a 300 K film injector near the wall.
  • Higher coolant flow produced roughly 30-40% lower average wall temperatures.

Created a MATLAB tool that converts the NACA four-digit definition into upper and lower surface coordinates, then uses numerical integration to estimate section area and wing volume.

2% Maximum camber
